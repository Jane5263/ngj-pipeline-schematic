The v2.4 Engine
Pipeline Schematic

A guided tour of how a candidate drug becomes a ranked, safety-checked therapeutic prediction.

Version 2.4 · 2026-04-15 1,783 drugs · 8 organs · 3 diseases Hover or tap · for detail

Overview

The engine predicts the effect of any candidate compound on diseased tissue and on eight major organs. A drug is mapped to its known protein targets; that perturbation is propagated through an 18,727-gene regulatory network calibrated against 1,116 menstrual reference samples; the result is a per-gene prediction of expression change for each tissue of interest. Two questions are scored against every prediction: does the compound move diseased tissue toward a healthy molecular state, and does it perturb any organ toward toxicity? Every engine release is gated against four real-world benchmarks — measured drug effects in patient cohorts and ex-vivo studies — before any candidate score is published.

Does it help? Will it hurt? — four parallel scoring lanes

Layer · Score

Given the prediction · is the drug good for the patient and safe for their organs?

The per-gene prediction is the input. Four scoring lanes turn it into numbers a human (or a ranking algorithm) can act on. Two of them grade efficacy (does the drug push the patient toward healthy?). One of them grades safety (does it push any organ toward toxic?). One of them enriches with what we already know about the drug from the literature.

The scoring vocabulary — what each number actually measures

Reference

When we say '0.826' or 'NES 2.74' · what is being measured?

The four scoring lanes produce a handful of named metrics. Each one answers a specific question with a specific number. Here's what they measure, how they're computed (no formulas, just intuition), and what a 'good' value looks like for each.

Efficacy metrics

Lane A · Does the drug move the patient toward health?

Tissue concordance

Pearson correlation · 15,000 genes
Asks
Does the drug's predicted gene-by-gene change look like moving the patient from sick toward healthy in this tissue?
How
Take the predicted change for every gene. Take the 'sick minus healthy' direction (the gap that needs closing). Compute their correlation. If positive, the drug is closing the gap. If negative, it's widening it.
Reads
A number between −1 and +1. Positive = restoring health. Higher is better.
DISCOVER psoriasis · AUC 0.749 for predicting Ustekinumab PASI75 response from baseline biopsies (n=67).

Menstrual alignment

Pearson correlation · 14,000 genes · IRS-matched
Asks
At this patient's inflammation level, does the resulting gene-gene structure look more like menstrual biology — our gold-standard health map?
How
The menstrual hub has a gene-gene correlation matrix for every inflammation window. Find the window matching the patient's IRS. Compare the predicted gene-gene structure to that menstrual reference. If they match, the drug is restoring menstrual-like balance.
Reads
Correlation, −1 to +1. Higher = more menstrual-like = healthier. Captures different biology than tissue concordance (only 46% shared variance).
DISCOVER · AUC 0.750 · combined with tissue concordance: AUC 0.766. The 'both-high' patient quadrant has a 92% PASI75 response rate (OR=23, p<0.0001).
Validation metrics

Used at the gate · how well do predictions match real-world drug effects?

NPA · Network Perturbation Amplitude

Adapted from Martin et al. 2014
Asks
After weighting genes by how central they are to the network (hub genes count more), how well does the prediction match what we actually measured?
How
Multiply predicted change by observed change, gene by gene. Weight each by that gene's connectivity in the network backbone. Sum it all up. The result is a signed concordance score — positive when prediction and reality agree, negative when they diverge.
Reads
Always reported with two p-values. O = gene specificity (would shuffling gene labels give the same score?). K = structure specificity (would using a random network give the same score?). Both p<0.002 means the score is really about this drug acting on this network.
Aspirin in real patients · NPA 0.826 (O,K p<0.002).
CDD-2728 ex vivo · NPA 0.892 in high-inflammation subset (pooled 0.839).

WES & NES · Weighted Enrichment Score

CMap / LINCS methodology
Asks
Are the genes the engine predicted would go up actually up in the data? And conversely for down?
How
In the observed data, rank every gene from most-up to most-down. Now look at the genes the engine predicted should go up — where do they sit in the ranking? If they cluster at the top, that's positive enrichment. WES quantifies it; NES (Normalized Enrichment Score) re-scales WES so values are comparable across experiments.
Reads
NES around 2 is meaningful enrichment. NES > 2.5 is strong. Positive NES = predictions match direction; negative = opposite direction.
Aspirin · NES 2.74.
CDD-2728 high-IRS · NES 2.57.
Ibuprofen · NES 2.15.
Safety & biological-state metrics

Lane B + Lane C · is the drug damaging anything, and what state is the tissue in?

Organ tox AUC

Per organ · validated against FDA labels
Asks
When we rank all drugs by predicted damage to a given organ, how well does that ranking separate known toxic from known safe drugs?
How
Each organ has a 'damage signature' — a reference perturbation pattern that represents injury. Every drug is scored against the signature. Then we compare against FDA labels (DILIrank for liver, DICTrank for heart, ChEMBL nephrotox for kidney, ChEMBL GI for colon) and compute the AUC.
Reads
1.0 = perfect ranking (every toxic ranks above every safe). 0.5 = random. The pattern across organs tells a story: kidney damage is mostly transcriptional — heart damage often happens through ion channels that don't change gene expression.
Kidney · AUC 0.776 (n=117, ChEMBL).
Liver · AUC 0.718 (n=132, DILIrank).
Colon · AUC 0.686 (n=148, ChEMBL).
Heart · AUC 0.620 (n=105, DICTrank).

Fibrosis composite v2.4

6 components · NASH-calibrated
Asks
Is the drug pushing the tissue toward scar formation?
How
Six fibrosis components are scored independently: fibroblast activation state (weight 0.633), persistence / ECM (0.337), hepatic stellate activation, fibrogenic cytokine signaling, resolution failure, ECM remodeling. They combine into a single composite.
Reads
Higher = more pro-fibrotic. Calibrated against 216 NASH liver biopsies (Kleiner F0-F4 staging).
AUC 0.734 separating Kleiner F0-1 from F2-4.
Composite correlation ρ = +0.410 with Kleiner stage.
Currently informational — reported alongside efficacy, not yet in composite ranking.

IRS · Inflammation Reference Score

Universal yardstick · cross-tissue
Asks
On a single scale that means the same thing in skin, blood, liver, gut, anywhere — how inflamed is this sample?
How
A trained mixer (IRS_mixed_v3) reads gene expression and outputs one number. Calibrated against the menstrual reference, so 'high inflammation in skin' means roughly the same biological state as 'high inflammation in blood'.
Reads
A continuous score. Patient samples shift higher than healthy controls. We slice it into 31 sliding windows for reference building. Used everywhere — every cohort, every drug response analysis.
The universal inflammation yardstick. Every reference map, every drug perturbation, every patient sample carries an IRS value.

Collapse drivers

6 methods · ranked gene list per disease
Asks
Which genes are the bottlenecks? If we could restore just a handful of genes, which ones would do the most to restore tissue health?
How
Six methods identify critical nodes in the diseased network: (1) Dynamic Network Biomarkers — genes whose co-expression explodes near disease transitions; (2) Eigenvector centrality — the network's biggest hubs; (3) WGCNA module preservation — modules that fall apart in disease; (4) Pathway preservation; (5) Granger causality — genes that predict others' changes; (6) Key Driver Analysis. Genes scoring high across multiple methods are flagged.
Reads
A ranked list per disease. The top-ranked genes are the most attractive drug targets — restoring them should restore the most downstream network structure.
Used in target prioritization for the v2.4 candidate shortlist — feeds into the 60%/40% composite weighting (60% target screen rank, 40% collapse score).

Reading the dashboards

When a candidate report shows "NPA 0.83 (O,K p<0.002) · WES NES 2.7 · tissue concordance 0.31 · menstrual alignment 0.28 · liver tox 0.21 · heart tox 0.08", you can now decode it: the drug looks real (NPA + NES are strong), it's modestly restoring tissue and menstrual structure (concordances positive), it's safe for heart (low) but watch the liver (moderate).

The validation gate — four real-world tests the engine must pass

Layer · Gate

Before we ship any change · do the predictions still match reality?

Predictions are easy. Predictions that match reality are rare. Every engine change is gated against four real-world benchmarks: two drugs whose effects on patients we've measured, and two metrics on the DISCOVER psoriasis trial. If any one regresses, the change doesn't ship.

Fig 5 · Four pillars hold up the gate. Each one is a published, measured drug effect. Each one must hold — re-simulated from scratch — for any engine change to ship.
Primary Gate · Mandatory

Every engine, parameter, or solver change must re-run scripts/run_live_validation.py end-to-end. None of the four scores above may regress.
